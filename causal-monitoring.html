The Causal Imperative

From correlation to causation

Predictive models tell you who is at risk. Causal models tell you why and what will work. ATLAS uses five complementary methods to build causal understanding at every level of the system.

Causal Methods

Five methods for causal inference

Each method addresses different data structures and identification strategies. The system automatically selects the appropriate method based on available data and the question being asked.

1. Granger Causality — Temporal Precedence

Temporal precedence test — Does knowing X’s past improve prediction of Y beyond Y’s own past?

  • Use case: Do attendance dips Granger-cause grade declines?
  • Method: VAR model with F-test on lagged coefficients
  • Lag selection: AIC/BIC with max lag of 6 grading periods
  • Output: Directed temporal influence graph with p-values

Limitations

Granger causality establishes temporal precedence, not true causation. Used as a screening tool to identify candidate causal relationships for deeper analysis.

2. Propensity Score Matching (PSM)

Observational quasi-experiment — Match treated and untreated learners on observable characteristics to estimate treatment effects.

  • Use case: What is the causal effect of tutoring on math scores?
  • Matching: Nearest-neighbor with caliper = 0.2σ of logit propensity
  • Balance check: Standardized mean differences < 0.1 on all covariates
  • Estimand: Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT)
  • Sensitivity: Rosenbaum bounds for hidden bias

Covariates

Prior achievement, attendance rate, FRL status, ELL status, IEP flag, grade level, school, prior year teacher effectiveness rating. 48+ features available from the feature store.

3. Instrumental Variables (IV)

Exogenous variation exploitation — Use an instrument that affects the treatment but not the outcome directly to estimate causal effects.

  • Use case: Causal effect of absences on achievement (weather as instrument)
  • First stage: Regress attendance on instrument + controls
  • Second stage: Regress outcome on predicted attendance
  • Validity tests: First-stage F > 10, exclusion restriction argument
  • Estimand: Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE)

Example Instruments

Weather severity for attendance, distance to school for transportation effects, lottery-assigned programs for program evaluation, policy discontinuities for class size effects.

4. Difference-in-Differences (DiD)

Policy evaluation design — Compare pre/post changes between treatment and control groups to estimate program impact.

  • Use case: Impact of new reading curriculum adopted by some schools
  • Assumption: Parallel trends in pre-treatment period
  • Validation: Placebo test with pseudo-treatment dates
  • Extensions: Staggered DiD (Callaway-Sant’Anna) for rollout programs
  • Estimand: Average Treatment Effect (ATE)

Parallel Trends

The system automatically tests parallel trends with event-study plots and reports pre-treatment coefficient magnitudes. If trends diverge, results are flagged with a credibility warning.

5. Bayesian Networks — Structural Causal Models

Structural causal models — Learn directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) representing causal relationships among variables.

  • Use case: Map the full causal structure among attendance, behavior, grades, and home factors
  • Structure learning: PC algorithm + domain expert priors
  • Parameter learning: Bayesian estimation with Dirichlet priors
  • Inference: do-calculus for interventional queries P(Y | do(X))
  • Visualization: Interactive DAG explorer for administrators

Advantages

Unlike single-equation methods, Bayesian Networks capture the entire web of causal relationships simultaneously. They answer “what if” questions and identify mediating pathways between intervention and outcome.

Divergence Detection

CUSUM change-point detection

Cumulative Sum control charts detect the moment a learner’s trajectory diverges from their projected growth path. Faster than waiting for end-of-quarter reports.

Detection Speed

CUSUM detects trajectory changes 3–5 weeks earlier than traditional threshold alerts. The cumulative sum accumulates small deviations that individually fall below alarm thresholds.

Adaptive Thresholds

Decision boundaries adapt to each learner’s historical variance. High-variance learners get wider bands to reduce false positives; stable learners get tighter bands for faster detection.

Bidirectional Monitoring

Both negative divergence (decline) and positive divergence (unexpected growth) are monitored. Positive divergence identifies students who may be ready for accelerated challenges.

Growth Projections

Bayesian growth modeling

Hierarchical Bayesian models project each learner’s growth trajectory with uncertainty quantification. Projections incorporate learner history, peer trajectories, and intervention effects.

Feedback Loop

Closed-loop learning system

ATLAS doesn’t just recommend — it tracks outcomes, updates its models, and learns which interventions actually work for which learner profiles.

Continuous Improvement: Each complete cycle (signal → intervention → outcome) generates a labeled training example. After 30+ observations per intervention type, the system can estimate causal effects with confidence intervals narrow enough for actionable decision-making.

Model Drift Detection

Population Stability Index (PSI) monitored weekly on feature distributions. PSI > 0.2 triggers automatic model retraining. Concept drift detected via ADWIN windowed comparison.

Fairness Monitoring

Equalized odds and demographic parity checked per model refresh. Disparate impact ratio monitored across race, gender, FRL status, ELL, and IEP subgroups. Violations trigger bias review board notification.
